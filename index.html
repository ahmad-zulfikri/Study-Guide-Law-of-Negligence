Evolution of Duty of Care

Historically, establishing a duty of care was complex. In Anns v Merton [1977], the court simplified it into a two-stage test. However, because stage one only required simple foreseeability of harm, it created a massive, unmanageable scope of liability. To correct this, the court overruled Anns and formulated the Caparo Three-Fold Test, introducing a third check: whether it is "fair, just, and reasonable" to impose a duty.

The Overruling Reason: The Anns test put excessive weight on broad policy arguments at the second stage to limit liability after the fact, rather than evaluating the authentic close relationship between the parties initially.

Maxim: Res Ipsa Loquitur

In specific situations, the plaintiff cannot directly prove what physical action the defendant took that was negligent, but the facts of the accident speak for themselves. Under Scott v London Docks, three conditions must be proven:

1. The object or apparatus causing the damage was under the exclusive control of the defendant.
2. The accident is of such a nature that it would not ordinarily happen without negligence.
3. There is an absence of an explanation (unknown cause) for the event.

Duty of Care [1932] AC 562

Donoghue v Stevenson

The Snail in the Ginger Beer Case

Facts: A friend purchased a bottle of ginger beer for the plaintiff. The plaintiff drank it and found a decomposed snail in the opaque bottle, falling ill.

Held: A manufacturer owes a duty of care to the consumer. Created the "Neighbor Principle" of proximity and reasonable foreseeability.

Duty of Care [1977] 2 All ER 492

Anns v Merton LBC

Structural Defects & Cracks Case

Facts: Cracks appeared in building foundations. Plaintiff sued the local council for failing to inspect the foundations properly.

Held: Established the Two-Stage Test. Later overruled because it made establishing a duty of care far too easy, over-relying on policy considerations to limit liability.

Duty of Care [1990] 2 AC 605

Caparo Industries v Dickman

Corporate Audit Discrepancy

Facts: An investment firm bought shares relying on inaccurate audited accounts, suffering severe financial loss.

Held: Established the modern Caparo Three-Fold Test: Foreseeability, Proximity, and whether it is Fair, Just, and Reasonable.

Breach of Duty [1971] 2 QB 691

Nettleship v Watson

Learner Driver Crash

Facts: A learner driver hit a lamp post during a lesson, injuring the instructor who was teaching her.

Held: A learner driver is held to the objective standard of an ordinary competent driver. Inexperience is not a defense.

Breach of Duty [1998] 1 WLR 1275

Mullin v Richards

School Play-Fight Accident

Facts: Two 15-year-old schoolgirls play-fought with rulers. A ruler snapped, and a fragment blinded one girl.

Held: A child's standard of care is judged against the standard of an ordinary child of the exact same age. Defendant was not negligent.

Breach of Duty [1957] 1 WLR 582

Bolam v Friern Hospital

Electroconvulsive Therapy Standard

Facts: Patient underwent shock therapy without relaxant drugs, fracturing his pelvis.

Held: Formulated the Bolam Test: A professional is not negligent if their action is supported by a responsible body of professional peers.

Causation [1968] 1 QB 428

Barnett v Chelsea Hospital

Arsenic Poisoning Factual Chain

Facts: A nightwatchman visited the clinic complaining of vomiting. The doctor refused to inspect him, telling him to go home. He died of arsenic poisoning.

Held: The doctor breached his duty, but the patient would have died from advanced poisoning anyway. The "But-For" test failed.

Remoteness [1961] AC 388

The Wagon Mound (No. 1)

Oil Spill and Dockyard Fire

Facts: Oil spilled from a ship ignited sparks from welding on a nearby dock, causing a catastrophic fire.

Held: Standardized the "Reasonable Foresight Test". The damage was too remote because the physical type of damage (fire) was not foreseeable.

Duty of Care [1991] 1 AC 398

Murphy v Brentwood District Council

The Defective Foundations & Economic Loss Case

Facts: The plaintiff bought a house that later developed severe structural cracks due to defective foundations. He sued the local council for negligently approving the faulty building plans, which caused him a significant financial loss when he had to sell the house below market value. .

Held: The House of Lords officially overruled Anns v Merton LBC. The court ruled that the local council owed no duty of care for "pure economic loss" (the financial loss from the defective building itself), severely restricting the expansion of negligence liability for local authorities.
